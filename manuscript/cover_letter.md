We are pleased to submit our manuscript, **"Effective-sample-size calibration
of a modality test for cluster homogeneity and the number of classes in
spatially autocorrelated data,"** for consideration as a research article in
*Mathematical Geosciences*.

Deciding how many classes are present is a basic step in the unsupervised
classification of spatial data, yet the standard internal criteria for this
choice — the gap statistic, the average silhouette, and the Calinski–Harabasz
and Davies–Bouldin indices — all assume independent observations. On spatially
autocorrelated fields that assumption fails, and we document that the
consequence is systematic over-detection: on autocorrelated fields containing
no discrete classes these criteria invent clusters in every one of 75
controlled realisations, and on real imagery they fragment single land-cover
regions.

Our contribution is to recast the problem in explicitly geostatistical terms.
We treat cluster-number selection as a sequence of homogeneity tests, asking at
each step whether a region carries multimodality in excess of its spatial
autocorrelation, and we make the test valid on autocorrelated fields by
calibrating it at the effective sample size. The construction rests on tools
central to the mathematical-geosciences community: the effective sample size
(Cressie; Dutilleul), a within-class integral range estimated by local
declustering (Isaaks & Srivastava; Deutsch & Journel), an adaptive
trend–residual decomposition in the universal-kriging spirit, and a
Gaussian-random-field neutral-model reference generated by spectral simulation
(Goovaerts; Dietrich & Newsam; Lantuéjoul). The resulting method, ESS-Dip,
applies Hartigan's dip statistic along a locally optimal split direction within
a recursive partitioning, and carries an asymptotic false-split-control
property.

We validate the method on a factorial simulation with known ground truth, where
it attains the best balanced score of thirteen criteria, with the highest
exact-*K* recovery among the methods that reach unit specificity; and on three
real scenes spanning hyperspectral (Indian Pines, Salinas) and multispectral
(Sentinel-2 with ESA WorldCover) sensing. We are deliberate about the method's
scope: it is a precision-oriented test of cluster homogeneity — a guard against
the over-segmentation the standard criteria exhibit, and a natural stopping rule
— rather than an exhaustive classifier, and we characterise this trade-off
honestly, including the cases where it under-reports. All code and data needed
to reproduce every result are released.

We believe the manuscript is a good fit for *Mathematical Geosciences*: it
brings the journal's core machinery — the effective sample size, declustering,
variogram modelling, the integral range, and Gaussian-simulation neutral models

reproducible method rather than an application of existing tools. [Code and
